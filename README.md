**1. Executive Summary**
Context: Analysis of financial health and operational scale for a healthcare center with 5,000 recorded visits from Jan 2024 to May 2025.

Assumption: Total billing is calculated as the sum of Treatment, Medication, and Room charges across all departments.

Insight: Total Revenue reached £3.36M. The Average Billing per Visit in 2025 stands at £709.42, achieving a solid 9.25% growth rate compared to the previous year (£649.38).

Analysis: The consistent revenue growth suggests a successful scale-up in high-value medical services or a more effective billing strategy in 2025. However, this financial success needs to be balanced against operational costs to ensure long-term sustainability.

**2. Doctor (Provider) Review**
Context: Evaluation of workload distribution and service efficiency among 5 primary providers (Drs. Abisola, Patel, Grek, Jones, and Kikiola).

Assumption: Provider performance is assessed based on patient volume (case count) and the average satisfaction score linked to their visits.

Insight: Patient load is evenly distributed, with each doctor handling approximately 1,000 patients. While financial productivity is stable, the revenue per provider varies based on the specific procedures performed.

Analysis: The high uniformity in patient volume indicates an excellent appointment distribution system. However, the variance in revenue per doctor suggests that certain providers are handling more complex, high-cost cases (like surgery or emergency care), which may lead to higher burnout risks compared to those in general consultation.

**3. Patient Review**
Context: Assessment of patient demographics across 40 cities and their feedback via satisfaction ratings (scale 1-10).

Assumption: Geographic analysis assumes that "City ID" correctly maps to the physical location of the patients served.

Insight: Despite positive financial growth, the Average Satisfaction Score is only 3.84/10. This highlights a critical disconnect between increasing billing rates and the actual patient experience.

Analysis: A satisfaction score below 4.0 is a red flag. The data suggests that as billing amounts increased (9.25%), the perceived value did not keep pace. This "Satisfaction Gap" could lead to patient churn in the future. Immediate investigation into wait times, facility quality, or communication issues is required to protect the hospital's reputation.
